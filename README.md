# What Are E-Bikes and Why Can They Be a Safety Concern for Young People?

## Introduction
E-bikes are bicycles fitted with an electric motor, a rechargeable lithium-ion battery, and a controller system. The motor assists the rider when pedalling, making travel faster and easier. Key components include the frame, motor (mounted near the pedals or rear wheel), battery pack, display screen, and brakes. In NSW, legal e-bikes are capped at 250 watts of power and the motor cuts out at 25 km/h. They look similar to regular bikes but are heavier and faster, which makes them harder to control, especially for younger riders.

## Why Young People Use E-Bikes
E-bikes have surged in popularity among young people because they are affordable, require no licence or registration, and provide a sense of independence. Students use them to travel to school and social activities without relying on parents. There are now an estimated 760,000 e-bikes in NSW, reflecting massive growth since 2022. Social media and peer influence have also driven uptake, with large group "rideouts" becoming common. For many young people, e-bikes also represent social status and freedom.

## Safety Risks and Injury Data
Despite their benefits, e-bikes are a significant safety concern. NSW recorded 226 e-bike related injuries in 2024, rising to 233 in just the first seven months of 2025. Sydney Children's Hospital reported nearly 100 children hospitalised from e-bike injuries in 2025, up from 60 the previous year. There have also been multiple fatalities, including four e-bike related deaths in NSW in 2025. Common injuries include head trauma, spinal damage, and loss of consciousness. Teenagers are the least likely age group to wear helmets correctly, which significantly increases injury severity. Illegal and modified high-powered bikes that exceed legal speed limits make crashes even more dangerous. Battery fires are also a growing risk, with NSW incidents rising from 23 in 2022 to 93 in 2024.

## Laws and Rules Around E-Bikes
E-bike riders in NSW must follow the same road rules as regular cyclists. This includes wearing an approved helmet, riding on the left side of the road, obeying traffic signals, and using lights at night. No licence or registration is required. Legal e-bikes must not exceed 250 watts, with motor assist cutting out at 25 km/h. The NSW Government is currently introducing a minimum riding age, likely between 12 and 16, along with new European safety standards for e-bike power and battery compliance. Police now have expanded powers to seize and crush illegal high-powered bikes.

## Personal Skills and Strategies for Safe Riding
Young people can make safer decisions by always wearing a properly fitted helmet, riding only legal and compliant bikes, and following all road rules. Avoiding carrying passengers, not using a phone while riding, and slowing down at intersections all reduce crash risk. It is also important to check that the battery and charger are certified to avoid fire hazards. Knowing where to seek help — such as Transport for NSW or Bicycle NSW — is also an important part of making responsible choices around e-bike use.

## Conclusion
E-bikes offer young people real benefits including independence, convenience, and environmentally friendly transport. However, rising injury rates, fatalities, and illegal modifications show they are a serious safety concern. By understanding how e-bikes work, following the road rules, and riding responsibly, young people can reduce their risk and make safer decisions for themselves and those around them.
